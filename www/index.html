To create an accessible and walkable city, it is important to first look at the city’s current landscape and identify specific areas for improvement. To do this, we have calculated the average Walk Score® for each census tract.

Walk Score's® consider the proximity of amenities and are an important factor when people look for places to live, work and visit.

Correlation between Walk Score® and Proportion of Population Walking

A strong correlation exists between the percentage of the population who walks as a primary method of commute and the area's Walk Score® when the walk score is 75 or higher, which can be seen through the steep incline of the slope.

These findings suggest a Walk Score® of 75 or above is essential to encourage walking among residents.

Connecting Walkable Communities through Transit

“Even if individual neighborhoods are walkable, if they are disconnected from one another and people still have to drive to get around, the overall walkability of the city is limited."

- Jeff Speck (renowned urban planner)

Accessible transit allows for the connection of one walkable census tract to another. Connectivity of walkable census tracts throughout Vancouver allows people to commute in a way where they can start and end their journeys on foot.

Population Growth Projection for the City of Vancouver 2023-2040

The projected population growth of each census tract within the City of Vancouver helps build a better understanding of which areas will experience the majority of this growth.

From this, we expect the highest growth around False Creek, with additional strong future growth in population around the downtown core and some of the neighbourhoods bordering the Fraser River.

Steps for Building a More Walkable City

To enhance walkability we suggest the following recommendations. First, effective walkability involves the consultation of community members. The city should then consider rezoning areas to allow for more mixed use buildings. Incentivizing these developments in key areas will also help to achieve this vision. All of this cannot be done without ensuring safe pedestrian infrastructure that makes urban mobility equitable for minority groups. The final piece of the puzzle involves accessible transportation to connect walkable communities together, creating a cohesive, walkable city.
